Every complex electronic device you use, from your phone to a satellite, started as an idea. But how does that idea become a physical, working piece of hardware? How do you move from a messy, unreliable breadboard prototype to a professional, robust, and manufacturable product?

The answer lies in Printed Circuit Board (PCB) design, and one of the most powerful, open-source, and accessible tools for this is KiCad.

---

## KiCad: Your Digital Workbench for Electronics

KiCad is a free, open-source software suite for Electronic Design Automation (EDA). Think of it as the "Photoshop" or "AutoCAD" specifically for electronics. It takes you through the entire design process:

* **Schematic Editor:** Draw your circuit's "blueprint." Place symbols for components and draw wires to show how they connect.
* **PCB Editor:** The place where the magic happens. Place component footprints and route the copper traces that connect them.
* **3D Viewer:** See a fully-rendered 3D model of your finished board to check for mechanical fit before manufacturing.

---

## From Breadboard to Board: What is a PCB?

Moving from a breadboard to a PCB is the single biggest step toward creating a professional, durable, and reliable electronic product. A PCB uses a "layer cake" of fiberglass and copper to connect components instead of a tangle of wires.

* **Substrate:** The rigid, non-conductive board (typically green FR-4 fiberglass).
* **Traces:** Thin copper lines etched onto the board that act as wires.
* **Pads:** Shiny copper spots where component legs are soldered.
* **Vias:** Small, plated-through holes that allow traces to connect from one layer to another.
* **Solder Mask & Silkscreen:** The protective coating (solder mask) and labeling (silkscreen).

## What You Will Learn in This Session

In this session, we build a complete AC-to-DC power supply project:

1. **Core Concepts:** Understanding the workflow from Schematic Capture to PCB Layout and generating manufacturing files.
2. **Master the Interface:** A guided tour of the Schematic and PCB Editors.
3. **Build Live:** We place component footprints, route copper traces, and create power planes.
4. **Design Rule Check (DRC):** Learn to catch errors, view the 3D model, and generate "Gerber" files.
